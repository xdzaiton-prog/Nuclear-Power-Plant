Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR)

How a Nuclear Power Plant Generates Electricity

Introduction to Nuclear Power

A nuclear power plant generates electricity using nuclear fission rather than burning fossil fuels. In a Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR), nuclear energy is converted into thermal energy, then mechanical energy, and finally electrical energy.

Nuclear Fission Process

Uranium-235 fuel undergoes nuclear fission when struck by a slow neutron. The nucleus splits into smaller nuclei, releasing heat energy, additional neutrons, and radiation. The neutrons sustain a controlled chain reaction inside the reactor core.

Primary Coolant System

High-pressure water circulates through the reactor core to absorb heat. The water is maintained at approximately 150 atmospheres, preventing it from boiling even above 300Â°C. This radioactive water flows through thick pipes to the steam generator.

Steam Generator (Heat Exchanger)

The steam generator transfers heat from the radioactive primary loop to a separate non-radioactive secondary loop. Thousands of metal tubes allow heat transfer by conduction, while preventing the two water systems from mixing. The secondary water turns into steam, which drives the turbine.

Steam Turbine and Generator

High-pressure steam expands through the turbine, converting thermal energy into mechanical rotational energy. The turbine shaft spins an electric generator, which produces alternating current through electromagnetic induction.

Condenser and Cooling System

After passing through the turbine, steam enters the condenser, where cooling water removes excess heat and converts it back into liquid. A third independent cooling loop transfers heat to the cooling tower, which releases water vapor into the atmosphere.

Safety Systems

The reactor is enclosed in a reinforced containment structure. Control rods regulate the chain reaction, and emergency cooling systems ensure reactor stability. The separation of three water loops ensures radioactive materials remain contained.
