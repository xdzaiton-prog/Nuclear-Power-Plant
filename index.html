Pressurized Water Reactor

A detailed exploration of how nuclear fission is controlled and transformed into reliable electrical energy using advanced reactor engineering.

Overview of Nuclear Power Generation

A Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR) is the most widely used type of nuclear reactor worldwide. It operates by using nuclear fission to generate heat inside a reactor core. This heat is transferred through a primary coolant system to produce steam, which drives a turbine connected to an electrical generator.

Reactor Core and Primary System

Nuclear Fission

Uranium-235 nuclei absorb slow neutrons and split into smaller nuclei, releasing energy, additional neutrons, and radiation. The reaction is carefully controlled to maintain steady power output.

Fuel and Control Rods

Fuel rods contain uranium pellets enclosed in zirconium alloy. Control rods made of boron or cadmium absorb excess neutrons to regulate the chain reaction and prevent overheating.

Primary Coolant Loop

High-pressure water (~150 atmospheres) circulates through the core. It removes heat while remaining in liquid form, then transfers this heat to the steam generator.

Steam Generator (Heat Exchanger)

The steam generator is a critical safety feature of the PWR design. It separates the radioactive primary loop from the clean secondary loop. Heat transfers through thousands of metal tubes without mixing the two water systems. The secondary water undergoes a phase change into high-pressure steam.

Energy Conversion Process

Steam Turbine

High-pressure steam expands across turbine blades, converting thermal energy into mechanical rotational energy.

Electric Generator

The turbine shaft spins a generator. Through electromagnetic induction, mechanical energy becomes electrical energy.

Condenser & Cooling

Exhaust steam is cooled and condensed back into water. A third cooling loop transfers excess heat to the cooling tower, maintaining system efficiency.

Safety Systems and Containment

PWR reactors are designed with multiple redundant safety systems. The reactor is enclosed within a reinforced containment building. Emergency Core Cooling Systems (ECCS), automatic shutdown mechanisms (SCRAM), and radiation monitoring systems ensure safe operation. The separation of three independent water loops prevents radioactive contamination.
